## April 2026 | ArryBarry Health & Beauty

## Executive Summary

The UK organic skincare market is in a period of strong recovery and growth following a brief downturn in 2023. Certified organic beauty and wellbeing products reached £151 million in sales in 2024 — an 11% year-on-year increase — after a dip to £136 million in 2023 caused by cost-of-living pressures. The broader UK natural and organic skincare segment is the fastest-growing within the wider £4.59 billion UK skincare market, projected to expand at a 6.82% CAGR through 2031. Consumer appetite for certified, transparent, and sustainably produced skincare continues to grow, particularly among Millennials and Gen Z.

## 2. Key Market Trends

### 2a. Ingredient Trends

**Bakuchiol** — The plant-derived retinol alternative from *Psoralea corylifolia* continues to lead. It delivers retinol-comparable anti-ageing benefits without the dryness, redness, or sun sensitivity. Strongly positioned as a "clean retinol" for sensitive skin. High-volume ingredient in certified organic formulations.

**Adaptogens & Neurocosmetics** — Ashwagandha, tremella mushroom, and ginseng are gaining mainstream traction. These help the skin "adapt to stress," support the skin barrier, and offer antioxidant properties. Predicted to be among the biggest ingredient stories of 2026.

**Microbiome-Friendly Formulations** — Pre- and post-biotics in topical skincare are moving from niche to mainstream. Consumer interest in the gut-skin axis is driving demand for products that support the skin's natural microbiome rather than disrupt it.

**Waterless Formulations** — Water-free (anhydrous) products reduce the need for preservatives, increase ingredient concentration, and significantly lower carbon footprint per unit. UK brand Vemel won 'Best Waterless Beauty Product' at a 2026 awards ceremony. Aligns with both sustainability and efficacy credentials.

**Upcycled Botanicals** — Ingredients derived from food or agricultural by-products (e.g., used coffee grounds, fruit stones, grain husks) are gaining significant attention, both for sustainability and the "circular economy" narrative. UpCircle Beauty has built an entire brand around this concept.

**Microalgae & Marine Actives** — Growing in organic formulations for hydration and anti-ageing, aligned with Elemis's established positioning around marine science.

**Exosomes** — An advanced biotech ingredient predicted to shape premium formulations in 2026, though not yet mainstream in certified organic products. ⚠️ REVIEW NEEDED — Compatibility with COSMOS certification is unclear for lab-derived exosomes.

**Food-Inspired Ingredients** — Consumer trust in ingredients they recognise from the kitchen (oat milk, turmeric, rosehip, sea buckthorn) continues to drive NPD.

### 2b. Consumer Behaviour Trends

- **Ingredient transparency is non-negotiable**: 81% of Gen Z consumers consider ingredient transparency when making purchasing decisions; they actively seek fragrance-free (81%) and paraben-free (33%) formulations.
- **Sustainability as a purchasing driver**: 78% of UK skincare buyers prioritise sustainability in their purchasing decisions. Sustainability information is now three times more important to consumers than product reviews or social proof.
- **Scepticism about green claims**: 79% of consumers are sceptical of brands' sustainability and social impact claims. This creates a strong advantage for certified brands.
- **Third-party certification as a purchase driver**: 41% of consumers cite third-party organic certification as a purchase motivator; 73% of shoppers are more likely to buy a certified product than a non-certified alternative.
- **Simplified routines**: Gen Z is increasingly favouring minimal, multifunctional products that address multiple concerns in one step.
- **Premiumisation despite cost-of-living pressures**: Premium players are outgrowing mass brands because efficacy-driven shoppers are willing to pay more for science-backed formulations. The "lipstick effect" sustains demand for small beauty indulgences even in tight economic conditions.
- **Social commerce**: 34% of social media users have purchased beauty products after discovering them via social platforms (TikTok and Instagram are primary channels).
- **"Inside-out" wellness**: Consumers are blending topical skincare with ingestible supplements, vitamins, and collagen drinks. Holland & Barrett has reconfigured its beauty category around this holistic approach.

### 2c. Sustainability & Packaging Trends

- **Plastic Packaging Tax**: Products with less than 30% recycled plastic content are now taxed in the UK, pushing brands to adopt recycled or alternative materials.
- **EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility)**: First fee invoices issued October 2025; Recycling Assessment Methodology (RAM)-based modulation active from 2026. Brands must classify packaging by 1 April 2026 to avoid enforcement action.
- **Refillable and zero-waste packaging**: Growing across all price points. UpCircle, Evolve, and Bybi are notable for recyclable or refillable formats.
- **Carbon neutrality claims**: Several UK organic brands (e.g., Evolve Organic Beauty) have achieved Carbon Neutral certification, increasingly used as a differentiator.
- **B Corp certification**: Growing as a trust signal alongside COSMOS/Soil Association. UpCircle, Bybi, Evolve are B Corp certified.
- **Greenwashing enforcement**: The CMA is actively enforcing under the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024. Vague claims like "good for the planet" now carry regulatory risk; brands need specific, substantiated claims.

### 2d. Technology & Innovation Trends

- **AI-powered personalisation**: Brands are increasingly using AI-driven skin analysis and product recommendation engines both online and in-store.
- **Direct-to-consumer (DTC) digital storytelling**: Online channels (45.12% revenue share in 2025) enable detailed ingredient storytelling that physical retail cannot match, particularly advantageous for certified organic brands with complex provenance narratives.

## 6. Consumer Demographics & Buying Behaviour

### Key Demographics

**Millennials (born 1981–1996)**
- Primary spending demographic for organic and premium skincare
- Highest online order frequency and annual beauty spend
- Value: efficacy, convenience, and quality. Reward brands that deliver results without gimmicks.
- Sustainability is important but must be paired with proven results
- Strong preference for trusted heritage brands alongside discovering new DTC brands

**Gen Z (born 1997–2012)**
- Fastest-growing organic skincare consumer segment
- **81% consider ingredient transparency** when purchasing
- **81% prefer fragrance-free** formulations; **33% prefer paraben-free**
- View clean beauty as a "non-negotiable," not a luxury
- Top purchasing priorities: inclusivity (43%), sustainability (19%)
- Seek cruelty-free, reef-safe, refillable products
- Heavily influenced by TikTok and Instagram; discover brands through social commerce
- Gen Z are often "skincare maximalists" (10+ step routines) OR "minimalists" — brands must speak to both
- Interested in skin microbiome health and science-backed natural actives

**Older Millennials and Gen X (35–55)**
- Core buyers for anti-ageing organic products (bakuchiol, peptides, facial oils)
- Willing to invest in premium certified formulations; less price-sensitive than younger cohorts
- Trust established heritage brands (Elemis, Neal's Yard, Liz Earle) and respond to clinical credentials

### Behavioural Patterns
- **Certifications matter**: 41% cite third-party certification as a purchase motivator; 73% are more likely to buy certified over non-certified
- **Scepticism of green claims**: 79% are sceptical of brand sustainability claims — driving the value of independent certification
- **Research-led purchasing**: Consumers are reading ingredient lists and checking certification status before purchasing, particularly online
- **"Inside-out" beauty approach**: Growing convergence of topical skincare and ingestible supplements, vitamins, collagen
- **Loyalty to values over brands**: Gen Z in particular will switch brands if perceived values misalign (especially on animal welfare, environmental responsibility)
- **Social media influence**: 34% of social media users purchased beauty products after seeing them on social platforms; TikTok and Instagram dominate for organic/clean beauty discovery

## 7. Regulatory & Certification Landscape

### COSMOS Standard (Primary UK/EU Organic Beauty Standard)
- Administered globally by COSMOS-standard AISBL (Brussels-based); UK certification delivered by Soil Association Certification
- **Two tiers**:
  - **COSMOS Organic**: Minimum 95% of physically processed agro-ingredients must be organic; at least 20% of the total formula must be organic (10% for rinse-off products)
  - **COSMOS Natural**: No specific organic percentage required but all ingredients must meet natural origin and processing rules
- **Version 4.2** came into effect **1 September 2025** (primarily editorial updates; no criteria changes in v4.1 or v4.2)
- COSMOS is the **leading global standard**: 73% of shoppers more likely to buy COSMOS-certified products
- **69%** of certified brands report that COSMOS certification boosted their sales

### Soil Association Certification
- UK's best-known and most trusted organic certification body
- Administers COSMOS Organic and COSMOS Natural in the UK
- Publishes annual **Organic Beauty & Wellbeing Market Report** (referenced throughout this document)
- Brands on the certified brand directory include Neal's Yard Remedies, Green People, Pai Skincare, Weleda, Evolve, and many others

### B Corp Certification
- Increasingly used as a broader sustainability trust signal alongside COSMOS
- UK organic brands with B Corp status in 2025: Evolve Organic Beauty, UpCircle Beauty, Bybi Beauty
- Growing consumer awareness of B Corp but lower recognition than Soil Association/COSMOS ⚠️ REVIEW NEEDED on latest UK consumer awareness data

### UK Greenwashing Regulation
- **CMA (Competition and Markets Authority)** actively enforcing Green Claims Code under new powers in the **Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCC)**
- Brands making vague claims such as "eco-friendly," "natural," or "good for the planet" without substantiation now face significant legal and financial risk
- **Requirement**: Claims must be specific, accurate, and independently verifiable
- This regulatory environment **favours certified brands** (COSMOS, Soil Association, B Corp) who can point to independently verified credentials

### Packaging Regulations
- **Plastic Packaging Tax (PPT)**: Products with less than 30% recycled plastic content are taxed. Rate increased in April 2025.
- **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**: Fee invoices issued from October 2025; Recycling Assessment Methodology (RAM) modulation from 2026. Brands must submit RAM classifications by **1 April 2026**.
- **Cosmetic Products (Restrictions) Regulations 2026 (proposed)**: Would ban Enzacamene in UV filters and other CMR substances; introduce formaldehyde-release labelling requirements ⚠️ REVIEW NEEDED on final legislative status.

### Microplastics Regulation
- EU ban on intentionally added microplastics is phasing in from 2023–2035; UK is following a parallel path. This affects glitter, exfoliating microbeads, and certain film-forming polymers.
- Organic brands already largely compliant by virtue of their formulation ethos; a potential competitive advantage as this bans more mainstream brands from certain formulations.

### Animal Testing
- UK maintains its own ban on cosmetics animal testing (aligned with previous EU position)
- Cruelty-free certifications (Leaping Bunny, PETA Beauty Without Bunnies) remain important purchase signals for organic skincare consumers

## 8. Key Opportunities & Strategic Considerations

*(Provided as context for ArryBarry market positioning — ⚠️ REVIEW NEEDED with commercial team before acting)*

1. **Certification as differentiation**: Given 79% consumer scepticism of green claims, independently certified products (COSMOS, Soil Association) have a credibility advantage that must be leveraged in all communications.

2. **Sensitive skin positioning**: The sensitive skin segment (led by Pai Skincare) shows strong growth and high loyalty. Fragrance-free, COSMOS-certified formulations addressing sensitive and reactive skin represent a high-opportunity niche.

3. **Price architecture gap**: There is a gap in the UK market between accessible organic brands (£8–£25) and premium certified brands (£40+). Mid-tier certified organic products (£25–£40) with strong clinical evidence represent an underserved space.

4. **Inside-out beauty**: The convergence of topical organic skincare and ingestible wellness (collagen, supplements) is a significant growth area. Holland & Barrett's category transformation reflects this shift.

5. **Retail channel strategy**: Holland & Barrett (wellness-led transformation), independent health stores, and DTC online represent the most natural distribution channels for new certified organic brands. John Lewis's beauty hall expansion offers premium positioning opportunities.

6. **Sustainability communications**: UK regulatory requirements mean that sustainability claims must be specific and substantiated. Lean into certification marks rather than generic language.

7. **Gen Z engagement**: Social-first (TikTok, Instagram), ingredient transparency, fragrance-free formulations, and minimal/multifunctional products are critical for this growing cohort.
